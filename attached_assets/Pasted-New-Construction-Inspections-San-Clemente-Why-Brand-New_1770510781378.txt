What Mold-Focused New Construction Inspections Cover
Here's what I check during new construction inspections San Clemente and Orange County when I'm looking specifically for mold risks. This goes way beyond what regular home inspectors check.
Foundation and crawl space moisture barriers - Is there a proper vapor barrier in the crawl space? Is it sealed at seams? Does it extend up the foundation walls? Are there tears or gaps? Is the crawl space ventilated properly? I've found new homes with no vapor barrier at all, or barriers installed so poorly they're useless.
Exterior moisture barriers - Is house wrap installed correctly with seams taped? Are windows and doors flashed properly? Are there gaps where water can get into wall cavities? In San Clemente, we need quality moisture barriers because of our ocean humidity and occasional storms.
Drainage systems - This is huge for preventing mold. Is grading correct so water flows away from the house? Are gutters and downspouts positioned right? Do they extend far enough from the foundation? Is there French drain or other drainage around the foundation? I've seen brand new Talega homes where water pools right against the foundation.
Ventilation systems - Are bathroom fans sized correctly for the room and our humid climate? Is there make up air in the home? Do they actually vent outside or dump into the attic? Is kitchen ventilation adequate? Is attic ventilation sufficient to prevent moisture buildup? Are crawl space vents positioned correctly?
HVAC and duct work - Is the system sized right for our climate where we need cooling but also humidity control? Are ducts sealed and insulated? Do they run through conditioned space or unconditioned attics? Are return vents balanced with supply vents? Poor HVAC creates humidity problems that lead to mold.
Bathroom waterproofing - Is the shower pan installed correctly? Is tile backer board used instead of regular drywall? Is grout sealed? Are bathroom walls properly waterproofed? I find waterproofing problems in probably 30% of new construction bathrooms I inspect.
Attic moisture and ventilation - Is insulation installed without gaps? Does attic have adequate intake and exhaust ventilation? Are there signs of moisture in the attic? Are bath vents terminating in the attic? Is there proper air sealing between the house and attic?
Materials assessment - Are materials appropriate for coastal environment? Some materials trap moisture, some resist it. Some hold up to salt air, some corrode. I check if the builder chose mold-resistant materials or just the cheapest option.
Construction moisture - This is something nobody thinks about. New construction has tons of moisture from concrete, lumber, joint compound, paint. Did that moisture dry out properly before the house was closed up? Or is it trapped in walls creating mold conditions?
For new construction inspections San Clemente, I also use moisture meters to test wood framing, check humidity levels throughout the house, and sometimes do air sampling if I suspect mold might already be growing.
Common Mold Risks in New San Clemente Construction and Remodeling
After inspecting homes here, I see these mold-promoting problems constantly.
Bathroom fans venting into attics - This is the number one issue I find. Maybe 40% of  construction has this problem. Humid bathroom air dumps into the attic, condenses on cold surfaces, grows mold. I've found attics with active mold growth in houses less than a year old.
Poor site drainage - Water pooling near foundations, gutters draining toward the house, improper grading. This puts constant moisture pressure on the foundation and creates damp conditions in crawl spaces and basements.
Inadequate attic ventilation - Attics need good airflow to prevent moisture buildup. Many new homes have undersized or poorly positioned vents. Combined with our humid marine layer, this creates perfect mold conditions in attics.
Windows and doors not flashed correctly - Gaps around windows and doors let water into wall cavities during rain. That moisture gets trapped, never dries out fully, grows mold inside walls.
HVAC issues - Systems undersized for humidity control, ducts not sealed leaking conditioned air into attics, return vents missing or inadequate. All of this creates humidity problems inside the house.
Construction moisture not dried - Builders close up houses before construction moisture has dried. Wet lumber, wet concrete, wet joint compound all trapped inside walls. This moisture has to go somewhere - and it often results in mold growth.
Cheap materials in wet areas - Regular drywall instead of moisture-resistant drywall in bathrooms. No waterproofing behind tile. Inadequate sealing around plumbing penetrations.
I inspected three new homes in the same Forster Ranch development last year. All three had bathroom fans venting into attics. All three had gaps in vapor barriers. All three had drainage graded slightly toward the house instead of away. The builder was making the same mold-promoting mistakes in every house they built.

The Inspection Process for New Construction Mold Prevention
For new construction inspections San Clemente focused on mold prevention, I recommend two inspections - pre-drywall and final.
Pre-drywall inspection happens after framing and rough-ins but before walls are closed. This is when I can see vapor barriers, ventilation ductwork, waterproofing, drainage, everything that prevents mold. Problems are cheap and easy to fix at this stage.
I check that vapor barriers are installed correctly with no gaps. That bathroom vents actually go outside with proper ductwork. That waterproofing is done right in showers and wet areas. That there's no trapped construction moisture in the framing. That drainage around the foundation is set up correctly. Make up air is set up.
Final inspection happens after the house is complete but before you close. This catches any moisture issues that developed during construction, checks that ventilation systems work properly, confirms humidity levels are normal, tests that drainage works as designed.
I use moisture meters to check wood framing moisture content. If its above 16%, thats a problem - means moisture is trapped somewhere. I use humidity meters to check if indoor humidity is appropriate. I sometimes do air sampling if I suspect mold might be present.
The pre-drywall inspection is critical for new construction. Once walls are closed up, you cant see whats behind them. If there are mold-promoting conditions behind those walls, you wont know until mold grows and you start seeing stains or smelling musty odors. By then, its expensive to fix.

Long-Term Mold Prevention in New Homes
Even with a good new construction inspection, new homes in San Clemente need ongoing attention to prevent mold.
I tell buyers to monitor humidity levels - keep it between 30-50%. Use bathroom fans during and after showers. Keep gutters clean. Check crawl spaces annually for moisture. Watch for any signs of leaks or water intrusion.
The first few years in a new home are critical. Thats when construction moisture is fully drying out. Thats when you'll discover if drainage is working correctly. Thats when any installation problems will start showing up.
I recommend new homeowners in San Clemente get a mold prevention assessment after the first year, then every 2-3 years after that. Just to make sure everything is working correctly and no moisture problems are developing.
New construction should be mold-free, but it takes proper installation and ongoing maintenance. With our San Clemente climate, you cant just ignore moisture issues and hope for the best.
