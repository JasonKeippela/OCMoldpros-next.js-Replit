## FAQ

### What is IAC2 certification and why does it matter?

IAC2 (Indoor Air Consultants International) is the recognized professional certification organization for mold inspection. IAC2 certified inspectors complete a structured training curriculum covering microbiology, building science, sampling methodology, health effects, and professional standards. Certification is maintained through continuing education. It's the credential insurance companies, attorneys, and real estate professionals recognize as the professional standard for mold inspection services.

### Can I verify your IAC2 certification independently?

Yes. OC Mold Pros' IAC2 certification is publicly verifiable at iac2.org. We provide our certification number in our written reports and can share it in advance upon request. Professional credentials should be transparent and independently verifiable — we agree.

### Why does it matter that you're inspection-only?

When an inspector also performs remediation, they have a direct financial incentive to find mold and recommend extensive work. An inspection-only business model eliminates that conflict entirely — our income depends solely on the quality of our inspection and documentation, not on whether remediation follows. This is the industry standard OC Mold Pros is built on.

### Does a certified mold inspection meet insurance company requirements?

Our reports are formatted to meet the documentation standards insurance adjusters expect — including professional credentials, moisture readings, photographic documentation, and laboratory analysis with chain-of-custody records. Most homeowner's insurance policies covering mold-related claims require professional assessment. We recommend confirming specific requirements with your insurer before the inspection.

### How is a certified mold inspection different from a general home inspection?

A general home inspection is a broad survey of all home systems performed in limited time. A certified mold inspection focuses specifically on moisture conditions, indoor air quality, and microbial risk using specialized tools and applied training that general home inspectors typically don't have. The two services are complementary, not interchangeable.
